The Prescription Opioid Addiction Epidemic

The Prescription Opioid Addiction Epidemic

Facts About Prescription Opioid Abuse

Prescription opioid addiction and abuse is a growing problem, with devastating consequences. Since 1999, sales of prescription opioids in the US has nearly quadrupled, while prescription opioid overdose deaths already have.
This correlation and others have not gone unnoticed, as medical agencies everywhere are making opioid abuse prevention a top priority.1

In 2015, almost 40% of Americans were using prescription painkillers2

The Pressure to Prescribe

100 million people are estimated to suffer from chronic pain in the US, leaving providers with the sometimes daily challenge of mitigating patient risk versus patient discomfort when prescribing opioids for pain relief. At times it may feel very difficult to make the right decision when it comes to your patients’ care.3,4

In 2015, 2 million Americans had a substance use disorder involving prescription pain relievers5

From Initial Prescription to Abuse

Many practitioners are familiar with the inherent qualities opioids possess that can easily contribute to patient abuse. Along with inducing feelings of euphoria and stress relief, these qualities include side effects such as tolerability (requiring an increase in dosing for similar effect) and withdrawal (when medication is stopped or withheld).

Even when opioids are taken eactly as prescribed, some patients may still become addicted. And not all patients take opioids as prescribed. For example, patients may take more than one dose at a time or pair their opioid medication with other medications that are contraindicated. Potential for abuse increases if patients have any common risk factors such as a history of mental illness or substance abuse.4

Nearly ½ of all U.S. opioid overdose deaths are due to prescription opioids6

What Prescribers Can Do to Help

As a prescriber, you have the ability to identify, address, and reduce prescription opioid abuse in your practice.7

  • Start by prescribing non-opioid pain relief whenever possible
  • If opioids appear necessary, begin with the lowest effective dosage
  • Check your state’s PDMP to monitor your patient’s prescribing activity
  • To avoid greater risk of patient death, don’t prescribe benzodiazepines along with opioids
  • Use the other resources available through Search and Rescue



4 out of 5 heroin users abused prescription opioids first8
